# Topic Types Reference

Every campaign is famous for *one* topic. The topic falls into one of four types. Mix them across a quarter so the audience gets variety — don't run four promotions in a row, don't run four product launches in a row.

---

## 1. Category theme

**What it is:** A usage occasion, flavour story, lifestyle moment, or product-category angle that ties multiple SKUs together under one banner. The campaign is famous for the *theme*, not any single SKU.

**When to use:**
- Between promotions to keep the list warm and engaged
- To reposition a product without a hard sell
- To open a seasonal moment (e.g., iced coffee season)
- To educate around a usage occasion the brand wants to own

**Examples for your brand:**
- "Iced Coffee Season" — feature Original Bean + Caramel + Iced Latte Kit
- "The 30-Second Morning" — feature the speed/ritual angle across all hero SKUs
- "Coffee Without Compromise" — comparison theme against pods/instant
- "Travel Coffee" — feature 60ml Travel Bottles + Mini Travel Pack
- "Dose Like a Barista" — feature the Jigger + the dosing ritual

**Famous-for framing test:** *"The campaign that makes your brand famous for [...]"* — if you can't fill that in cleanly, the theme isn't sharp enough.

**Common mistake:** Themes that are too broad ("Coffee at Home"). Tighten until the theme has a clear point of view.

---

## 2. Product launch

**What it is:** A new SKU, restock, new flavour, new bundle, or new format. The campaign is famous for *the product*.

**When to use:**
- Any time a real new product hits
- Significant restocks of a sold-out hero SKU (use sparingly — restocks aren't always launch-worthy)
- New bundles/kits as long as there's a genuine new combination

**Examples for your brand:**
- New flavour launch (Caramel was one — could have another flavour like Mocha, Hazelnut)
- New format (sachets, ready-to-drink can, larger bottle)
- New bundle (Iced Latte Kit was a launch-worthy bundle)
- New accessory (frother, jigger upgrades, new travel bottle)

**Famous-for framing test:** *"The campaign that introduces [product] as the [defining angle] from your brand."*

**Common mistake:** Launching a SKU without an angle. "We have a new flavour" isn't a campaign — "Caramel: the slow-drip dessert latte at home" is.

---

## 3. Calendar event

**What it is:** A campaign anchored to a date in the cultural or commercial calendar. The campaign is famous for *the occasion* — but it must connect to the brand authentically.

**When to use:**
- Real anchor dates with shopping intent (Mother's Day, Father's Day, Christmas, Valentine's, EOFY)
- Niche dates that suit the brand (International Coffee Day Oct 1, World Iced Coffee Day, etc.)
- Cultural moments where your brand has genuine relevance (winter coffee, summer iced)

**Examples for your brand:**
- Mother's Day Gift Pack push
- Father's Day "coffee on tap" angle
- Christmas — Gift Cards + Gift Pack + Latte Kit
- Valentine's — "share a dose" duo bundle angle
- EOFY — "upgrade your morning"
- International Coffee Day — heritage / brand story moment

**Famous-for framing test:** *"The campaign that owns [occasion] in the [category]."*

**Common mistake:** Forcing every calendar event. If the occasion doesn't actually fit the brand (e.g., Halloween for a coffee brand), skip it.

---

## 4. Promotion

**What it is:** A discount, free shipping offer, BOGO, threshold offer, or sale. The campaign is famous for *the deal*.

**When to use:**
- Strategic discounting moments (Black Friday, Click Frenzy, EOFY)
- Seasonal clearance
- Customer acquisition pushes (new-customer offer)
- Inventory pressure on specific SKUs

**Examples for your brand:**
- Black Friday / Cyber Monday
- Click Frenzy (Nov)
- EOFY
- "Stock up before [event]" (e.g., before summer iced coffee season)
- New-customer / win-back offers

**Famous-for framing test:** *"The campaign where your brand offers [specific deal] for [reason]."*

**Common mistakes:**
- **Back-to-back promos** — the single most damaging pattern. Promos lose force when stacked. Space them 3–4 weeks apart minimum.
- **Promo without a reason** — "20% off because" trains customers to wait. Anchor every promo to a calendar moment, inventory reason, or audience moment (new-customer, VIP-only).
- **Discounting hero SKUs constantly** — erodes the value of the brand. Promo less, premium more.

## Cross-quarter mix

A healthy quarter looks roughly like:

- 4–6 themed campaigns
- 1–2 product launches (if there are real new products)
- 1–2 calendar events
- 1–2 promotions

For 12 weeks at 2 campaigns/week, that's 24 sends total, organised into ~6 campaigns. That leaves room for flow emails, transactional emails, and the occasional ad-hoc note.

If a quarter has > 3 promotions in it, you're discounting too much. If it has 0 themes, the list isn't being entertained.

---

## Decision flow for picking the topic type

```
Is there a real new product to launch?
  → Product launch

Is there a calendar event in the next 2-4 weeks that fits?
  → Calendar event

Have we run a promotion in the last 2-3 weeks?
  → NO promotion — pick themed or calendar

Is the list warming or cooling?
  → Cooling → themed (re-engage) or promo (with reason)
  → Warm → themed (build affinity) or launch

Default if nothing else fits?
  → Themed campaign with a fresh angle
```

## ICP fit — match the theme to the persona

From the brand-intelligence skill, the three personas are:

- **Reformed Pod User** — themes that contrast against pods (sustainability, value, "without compromise")
- **Caffeine Optimiser** — themes around iced coffee, cost per cup, daily ritual
- **Traveller / On-the-Go** — themes around travel, work, adventure, the 60ml bottle

A strong campaign theme speaks to one persona clearly. A weak campaign theme tries to address all three at once and ends up generic.
