# Campaign Series Reference

Two series structures, depending on campaign type. Themed/Launch/Calendar campaigns use the 4-email arc. Promotion campaigns use the 7-email promo arc.

The golden rule across both: **every email stands alone**. Recipients may only see one. No "as mentioned last week", no "in our next email". Every email contains the full hook, the proof, and the reason to click.

---

## A. Themed / Launch / Calendar — 4-email arc (Give-Give-Give-Ask)

The shorthand is Give-Give-Give-Ask. The reality is **every email sells** — the Asks just get progressively stronger. A reader who only ever opens email #2 should still encounter a product and a reason to buy.

### Email 1 — Launch / Introduce (Give)

**Job:** Hook the reader on the theme. Make them feel "this is for me, this is timely, I want more."

**Structure:**
- Banner that signals the theme visually
- Opening hook (1–2 sentences max) — why this topic, why now
- Short body (3–5 sentences) — what the campaign will explore
- Soft CTA — "Shop the [theme]" or "See the [hero product]"
- Product strip (2–4 products tied to the theme)

**Sell mechanism:** introduces the theme but already routes click traffic to the relevant collection.

**Standalone test:** If this is the only email they read, do they know what we're talking about, why we care, and what to do next? Yes.

### Email 2 — Educate / Inform (Give)

**Job:** Teach something useful about the theme. Earn the reader's trust by giving them context that makes them better/smarter, not just more interested in buying.

**Structure:**
- Usually text-only or banner-light
- Educational hook (a question, surprising fact, or counter-intuitive claim)
- Body that delivers on the hook — 4–8 sentences
- Reference the product naturally as the proof point or solution
- Soft CTA — "Try it yourself" / "See how"

**Sell mechanism:** the education positions the product. The CTA still links to a purchase moment.

**Standalone test:** A reader who hasn't seen email #1 should still understand the theme from the first paragraph.

### Email 3 — Proof / How To (Give)

**Job:** Show the theme working in the real world. Customers using it, recipes, how-to, before/after, comparisons.

**Structure:**
- Banner showing the proof (user photo, product in use, recipe shot)
- Lead with the proof element (a review, a UGC quote, a quick recipe)
- 3–5 sentences of supporting context
- Stronger CTA — "Make it yourself" / "Shop the kit"
- Product strip with the products needed for the proof scenario

**Sell mechanism:** social proof or demonstration de-risks purchase. CTA is more confident.

**Standalone test:** Even the reader who's never seen the theme set up can read this proof email and understand both the theme and the offer.

### Email 4 — Product & Offer (Ask)

**Job:** Close. Strongest sell of the series. If there's an offer, this is where it lives.

**Structure:**
- Banner with the offer/product hero
- Direct, confident lead (no warmup)
- Reason to act now (limited time, limited stock, end of theme window)
- Single primary CTA — biggest button in the series
- Optional FAQ or objection-handler block at the bottom

**Sell mechanism:** explicit. This is the Ask.

**Standalone test:** A reader landing here cold should still get the theme, the value, and the reason to act — without needing the previous three emails.

### Default 2-per-week cadence (4-email arc)

- Week 1 — Email 1 (Tue), Email 2 (Fri)
- Week 2 — Email 3 (Tue), Email 4 (Fri)

Adjust send days to match the brand's historical best-performing send windows (check Klaviyo).

### Example arc — "Iced Coffee Season"

1. **Launch** — *"Iced coffee season is here"* — banner of iced latte, "summer's coffee is cold and ours is 30 seconds away", links to coffee collection
2. **Educate** — *"Why iced lattes taste worse at home (and how to fix it)"* — explains dilution, weak shots, the role of concentrate, soft link to Original Bean
3. **Proof** — *"Sarah from Brisbane swapped her iced latte habit"* — customer story + recipe card, link to Iced Latte Kit
4. **Offer** — *"Iced Latte Kit, $10 off until Sunday"* — direct offer, hero product, urgency

## B. Promotion — 7-email arc

Promotions compress. The 7 emails usually run over 7–14 days, not 3.5 weeks. Cadence rule bends for promos — you might send daily in the last 3 days.

### Email 1 — Early Access

**Job:** Reward loyalists / VIPs with first dibs. Build anticipation among everyone else.

**Audience:** VIP segment, recent buyers, top 10% engaged.

**Structure:**
- Simple banner with the offer
- One-line statement of what they're getting and why they're first
- Single button CTA — "Shop early access"
- No product grids, no scrolling. Get in, get out.

**Template:** Banner + CTA + text.

### Email 2 — Launch Offer

**Job:** Sale is live for everyone. Maximum clarity, minimum friction.

**Audience:** Full list (minus suppression).

**Structure:**
- Banner: clearly states what the sale is, how much off, on what
- CTA button — "Shop the sale"
- One short paragraph of supporting copy (what's included, end date)

**Template:** Banner + CTA + text.

### Email 3 — Sale Products

**Job:** Show them what's actually on offer. Reduce decision friction.

**Structure:**
- Banner restating the sale
- 4–8 hero SKUs in a product grid with discounted prices visible
- Short intro line ("Our top sale picks")
- CTA per product OR one CTA at the bottom

**Template:** Banner + text + products.

### Email 4 — Category Sale / Banners

**Job:** For broader catalogues, let the customer self-route. Avoid forcing one product.

**Structure:**
- Hero banner
- 2–4 category banners or large image-link blocks (e.g., Coffee / Kits / Accessories / Gifts)
- Brief copy under each — what's on offer in that category
- CTA per category

**Template:** Banner + text + products (using product-style blocks for categories).

### Email 5 — Best Sellers

**Job:** Social proof close-up. "If you're not sure, here's what everyone buys."

**Structure:**
- Banner with the sale + "Bestsellers" framing
- Top 4–6 bestselling SKUs (in this sale or all-time)
- Star ratings / review snippets visible
- Single CTA at the bottom

**Template:** Banner + text + products.

### Email 6 — Ends Soon (48 hours)

**Job:** Urgency. Pull the people who've been thinking about it.

**Structure:**
- Banner: "Ends in 48 hours"
- Single sentence reminder of the offer
- Single CTA — "Shop now"
- Maybe 2–3 top SKUs but keep it minimal

**Template:** Banner + CTA + text.

### Email 7 — Last Chance (ends tonight)

**Job:** Final push. Strongest possible urgency.

**Structure:**
- Often text-only for raw urgency feel — like a quick note from the founder
- Subject line typically the strongest: "Last chance" / "Ends tonight" / "Final hours"
- 2–3 sentences. No fluff. Direct.
- Single CTA — "Shop now before it's gone"

**Template:** Text only.

### Default promo cadence (compressed)

For a 10-day promo:

- Day 1 — Early Access (VIP only)
- Day 2 — Launch Offer (full list)
- Day 4 — Sale Products
- Day 6 — Category Sale / Banners
- Day 8 — Best Sellers
- Day 9 — Ends Soon (48 hr)
- Day 10 — Last Chance (ends tonight)

For shorter (5-day) promos, drop Email 4 or Email 5 and compress further.

### Critical promo rules

- **Don't promise what you can't deliver.** End-date in copy must match Shopify's actual discount code expiry.
- **Don't shadow-extend.** If the offer was "ends tonight" and gets extended, send a separate apology/extension email — never silently extend.
- **Inventory check before send.** Email 5 (Best Sellers) is dangerous if the hero SKU sells out mid-send.

## Subject line and preview text guidance (both series)

**Subject line targets:**
- < 50 characters (mobile preview)
- 3 variants per email for A/B testing
- Mix of styles: curiosity / benefit / specificity / urgency
- No clickbait — the subject must reflect what's in the email

**Preview text:**
- Complements the subject, doesn't repeat it
- 80–120 characters
- Often the strongest hook after the subject — don't waste it on "View this email in your browser"

**Subject-line styles to mix across a campaign:**

| Style | Example pattern | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| Curiosity | "Why iced lattes taste worse at home" | Education emails |
| Benefit | "Cafe-quality coffee in 30 seconds" | Launch emails |
| Specificity | "Sarah saved $480/year with your brand" | Proof emails |
| Urgency | "Ends tonight: 15% off everything" | Final-push promo emails |
| Question | "What's actually in your instant coffee?" | Education emails |
| Founder-direct | "A quick note from [founder name]" | Text-only personal emails |

Avoid heavy emoji use unless tested. One emoji at most per subject. Never use clickbait punctuation (!!!).
