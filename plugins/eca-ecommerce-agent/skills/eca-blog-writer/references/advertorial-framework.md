# Advertorial Framework (any angle)

Use this whenever the user asks for an **advertorial**, an "advertorial blog post", a "PR / news-style" piece, a "founder story", an "our story" page, or any story-led / news-style sales page. An advertorial is a distinct, high-converting format. Follow this framework closely.

## What an advertorial is

An advertorial is long-form, story-led or news-style **direct-response copy** dressed in editorial clothing. Its job is to convert **cold traffic** (usually paid ads) by grabbing attention with a hook, building trust and desire, then converting with stacked proof and repeated CTAs.

Two choices define every advertorial:

- The **angle** (the lead and the voice): founder story, news/PR, personal review, exposé, and so on. **The founder story is only one angle. Do not default to it.** Pick the angle that best fits the product, the audience, and the proof you actually have.
- The **hook** (the big idea the whole piece hangs on).

How it differs from the seven SEO/GEO blog styles:

- **Goal:** conversion from paid traffic, not organic ranking. The keyword-volume gate and the Search Console indexing step are **optional** here.
- **Publish target:** usually a Shopify **Page** (`/pages/...`), not a blog article. Confirm page vs blog article at Gate 1 (see `shopify-publishing.md`).
- **Compliance:** it **must** carry an advertising / marketing disclosure and a clear "this is an advertisement, not a news article" line. Non-negotiable.
- House rules still apply: **no em-dashes**, brand voice, approve angle + hook + headline at Gate 1, review the full draft at Gate 2.

---

## Step 1 (at Gate 1): pick the angle, then craft the hook

### Choose the angle (the lead and voice). Recommend 1 to 2 options, then confirm with the user.

- **Founder story** (first person). Origin / underdog journey. Best when the founder's story is compelling and differentiating. Full structure in Angle A below.
- **News / PR / investigative** (third person, journalist voice, news hook). "New Australian [product] is selling out as [audience] ditch [old solution]." Best when there is a timely peg, a category shift, a strong mechanism, or a striking data point. Full structure in Angle B below.
- **Personal review / "I tried it"** (first person reviewer). Someone tries it and reports the experience and results. Best when lived experience and before/after sell it.
- **Problem / exposé** ("The real reason [problem] won't go away", "What the [category] industry won't tell you"). Best when there is a myth to bust or a hidden mechanism to reveal.
- **Listicle news** ("5 reasons [product] is taking over [category] in 2026"). Skimmable, curiosity-led.

Pick ONE primary angle. You can borrow elements from others, but the lead and voice should be consistent.

### Craft the hook (the big idea)

The hook is the single compelling idea the whole piece hangs on. Build it from one or more of:

- **News peg / timeliness** (a season, trend, event, or "2026" recency).
- **A surprising stat or result** (a number that stops the scroll).
- **A contrarian or myth-busting claim** (challenges what people assume).
- **A unique mechanism** (the "reason why" it works that nobody else explains).
- **A transformation** (a dramatic before / after).
- **Curiosity / open loop** (a question or partial reveal that demands the click).
- **Authority / social proof** (experts, awards, big numbers, "selling out").

A strong hook is specific, fresh, and believable. The headline = the hook expressed through the angle. Run the **CTR test**: would it out-click what is already in the feed or the SERP? If not, sharpen it.

## The shared advertorial backbone (every angle moves through this)

1. **Hook headline** (plus, for news/PR, a dateline and byline) and a small **"Advertorial" label**.
2. **Lead** that delivers the hook in the first lines. No slow warmup.
3. **The problem, agitated**, with the named villain / alternative (the expensive, risky, or ineffective option).
4. **The discovery / mechanism** (the "reason why" it works). This is the heart. Explain the unique mechanism plainly and convincingly.
5. **The product as the answer**, with bolded scannable benefits and transparency (ingredients / specs).
6. **Proof, stacked**: numbers, awards, press, named "Verified Buyer" testimonials that handle objections, before / afters.
7. **A comparison vs the alternative** on the buyer's real criteria (cost, safety, time, ease).
8. **Repeated CTAs** (2 to 4 through the page) and a **simple usage demo** to kill the effort objection.
9. **Curiosity / scarcity close** ("are you ready to ...", "check availability").
10. **The required legal disclaimer** (advertisement + marketing disclosure).

The angle changes the lead, the voice, and the framing. The backbone stays the same.

## Angle A: Founder story (structure)

First person, the founder telling their own story.

1. "Advertorial" label + a news/authority headline with a transformation ("[Unexpected person] [achieves outcome] by [doing the thing]").
2. Byline + date + location.
3. Credibility snapshot (3 to 4 proof bullets).
4. **"How it all started"** (the problem / origin): the founder's specific, relatable pain; introduce the villain (e.g. the expensive or risky alternative) and why it failed them; end on the unmet need.
5. **"How I got started"** (the solution is born): introduce the product as the answer; bolded benefits; ingredients / specs for transparency.
6. Mid-article CTA block.
7. Trust / quality deep-dive; restate the social-proof number.
8. **"My biggest challenges"** (the underdog struggle): bootstrapping, sacrifices, family pitching in, the milestone of scaling. This is the likeability engine.
9. Recognition / awards (real only).
10. **"What's next"** (forward-looking, customer-focused).
11. **"How to use"** (simple 2 to 4 step demo).
12. Comparison vs the alternative.
13. Stacked named testimonials.
14. Final CTA.
15. Legal disclaimer.

## Angle B: News / PR / investigative (structure)

Third person, neutral-to-enthusiastic reportage. Reads like sponsored content / native advertising.

1. **News headline** = hook + news framing. Patterns: "New Australian [product] is selling out as [audience] ditch [old solution]", "Why [demographic] are switching to [product]", "[Surprising result]: how [product] is changing [category]".
2. **Dateline + byline**: "[CITY], [Month Year]  |  by [Staff Writer / Name]". An editorial cue. Use a generic byline. **Do not impersonate a real journalist or a real news outlet.**
3. **Lede**: the news hook in 1 to 2 sentences. What is happening and why now. Lead with the most newsworthy element.
4. **Context / the problem**: report the market problem or the trend, with the named alternative as the foil. Use real data and sources where possible.
5. **The investigation ("what we found")**: explain the mechanism, the "reason why" it works. This is where a news advertorial shines, it "reports" on how the product works. Quote real, on-the-record sources (the founder, the brand, a genuine expert or study). **Only real, attributable quotes and sources. Never invent an expert, a study, or a quote.**
6. **The product as the subject of the story**: third person, benefits, transparency, what makes it different.
7. **Proof**: sales / scale, awards, press, ratings, named testimonials.
8. **Comparison vs the alternative.**
9. **How to get it / availability**: CTA with curiosity or scarcity framing.
10. **Legal disclaimer.**

(For **personal review** and **exposé** angles, use the same backbone: a first-person reviewer lede or a myth/secret lede respectively, then problem, mechanism, product, proof, comparison, CTA, disclaimer.)

## Keep it evergreen: the auto-updating date

Advertorials convert better when they look current. Make any date on the page (the dateline, "Updated [Month]") **update itself to the current month and year** every time someone loads it, so the piece never goes stale. Two ways:

**Option 1, inline script (works in a page body on any theme).** Give the date a span with a sensible fallback, then update it on load:

```html
[CITY], <span id="adv-date">June 2026</span> &nbsp;|&nbsp; by [byline]
<script>
(function(){var m=["January","February","March","April","May","June","July","August","September","October","November","December"];var d=new Date();var el=document.getElementById("adv-date");if(el){el.textContent=m[d.getMonth()]+" "+d.getFullYear();}})();
</script>
```

The fallback text inside the span (e.g. the month you wrote it) shows if scripts are disabled; otherwise it is replaced with the live month and year. Note: Shopify does **not** evaluate Liquid inside a Page's body content, so use this script, not a Liquid filter, when the date lives in the body.

**Option 2, Liquid in a custom theme template (advanced, no JavaScript).** If the brand uses a dedicated page template, put `{{ "now" | date: "%B %Y" }}` in the template Liquid. This renders server-side (visible to crawlers) but needs theme access.

For most advertorials, use Option 1. Keep claims and any time-bound facts honest even as the date rolls forward (do not let "this month" imply a fresh event that did not happen).

**Byline:** advertorials often use a light, on-brand persona byline rather than a real name (for example "by Coffee Addict", "by The [Brand] Team"). Keep it honest: do not attribute the piece to a real journalist or a real publication.

## Discovery and indexing (advertorials work differently from blog posts)

An advertorial is fed by **paid traffic**, not organic search. Make this clear to the user and handle it differently from the seven blog styles:

- **The traffic source is the ad.** The page is reached by a Meta or Google ad whose creative links straight to the page URL. That is the "link". It does not need to live in the site nav or be linked from other pages.
- **It is orphaned by design.** Do not add it to the main navigation or link it from products/blogs unless the user specifically wants it to earn organic traffic too. With no internal links, search engines are unlikely to discover it on their own.
- **Usually do NOT submit it to Search Console, and consider `noindex`.** Most operators keep advertorials out of the organic index so the bold, news-style framing does not become the brand's organic presence and so Google does not treat a thin ad page as low quality. Skip the Phase 6 indexing step for advertorials. If the user wants it firmly excluded, add a `noindex` robots meta, which must sit in the page `<head>` (a meta tag in the page body is ignored), so it requires a theme edit (a conditional in `theme.liquid` / a custom page template) or an SEO app, not the page body.
- **The opposite choice is valid.** If the user wants the advertorial to also rank for its hook, then link it internally and submit it to Search Console. Make this an explicit decision, not a default.

Contrast with the blog styles (1 to 7), which are built for organic: those should be internally linked and submitted to Search Console (Phase 6).

## Compliance and honesty (all angles, non-negotiable)

- Label the piece an advertisement and include the marketing / advertising disclosure.
- **Do not** impersonate a real news outlet or real journalist, fabricate quotes, invent experts or studies, or fake awards, press, or testimonials. Use only real, attributable, substantiated material.
- Every claim must be truthful and supportable, especially health, beauty, and efficacy claims. Follow the brand's compliance rules and local advertising law (e.g. ACCC / TGA in Australia, FTC in the US). When unsure about a claim, soften it or leave it out.
- No em-dashes anywhere. First person for founder and review angles; third person for news/PR. Approve angle + hook + headline at Gate 1; review the full draft at Gate 2.

## What to gather at Gate 1 (all angles)

- The **angle** and the **hook** (recommend options, then confirm).
- The **problem** and the **named alternative / villain**.
- The **mechanism** (the "reason why" it works).
- The **product** plus 3 to 5 benefits and specs / ingredients.
- **Real proof**: customer count, awards, press, ratings, named reviews (pull from the brand's review platform), before / afters.
- The **offer** and the **CTA destination**.
- For news/PR: any **real** data, sources, or on-the-record quotes available (real only).

Never fabricate any of the above. If a proof point or quote does not exist, leave it out.

## Publish + house rules

Default publish target is a Shopify **Page** (`pageCreate`); confirm page vs blog article at Gate 1. Default to draft (`isPublished: false`) for review. The body carries the product-insert / CTA blocks and the **required disclaimer**. No em-dashes. Both approval gates apply.
