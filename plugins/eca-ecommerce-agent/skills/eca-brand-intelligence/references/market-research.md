# Deep Voice-of-Customer & Market Pain-Point Research

A repeatable method for gathering **external** brand intelligence — what real consumers say, in their own words, about the category and the products they currently use. This is the research step that makes a brand's market position evidence-based instead of assumed. It runs inside `SKILL.md` → Mode A, but can also be run on its own to produce a standalone market-research report.

---

## The role to adopt

> Act as a highly experienced **market research analyst** who specialises in consumer market research and opportunities.

Research the `[brand category]` in depth — specifically:
- the **pain points** the market has,
- the **problems with the solutions that currently exist**, and
- **what consumers are looking for** to solve those pain points.

The goal is to understand exactly where `[product type]` fits, and what problem it solves that the alternatives fail to. The output trains the marketing team and arms them with real language to promote the product.

---

## Source priority — real talk over industry papers

**Prioritise places where consumers openly discuss the category, unprompted.** Discussion-forum research gives the realest insight and the most leverage. In rough priority order:

1. **Reddit** — subreddits for the category, the problem, and competitor brands. The highest-signal source; search for complaint threads, "why does X suck", "best alternative to Y", and recommendation requests.
2. **Quora** — question-and-answer threads expose the exact questions and objections buyers have.
3. **Amazon / retailer reviews** — especially 1–3 star reviews of competing products; these are pain points stated plainly.
4. **X / Twitter** — real-time gripes, hot takes, and viral complaints or praise.
5. **Niche discussion forums** — category-specific communities (often the deepest expertise).
6. **Medium / blogs / YouTube comments** — long-form opinion and the comment sections under it.
7. **Review platforms** — Trustpilot, ProductReview (AU), Judge.me widgets on competitor sites.

Use industry/market-sizing reports **only** for the macro picture (size, growth, trends) — never as the source of customer insight. If a source can't be fetched directly, work from search results and snippets; respect platform and tool access rules and don't try to route around them.

> Note on tooling: surface threads with web search, then fetch the ones you're allowed to. Some of these sites are JavaScript-heavy or gated — if a fetch returns an empty shell, rely on the search snippet or a cached summary rather than guessing, and capture what you genuinely found.

## What to extract (and how to record it)

For every insight, capture the **verbatim quote**, the **source/platform**, and **rough recency/region** where visible. Group findings into:

1. **Pain points** — the recurring frustrations with the category as it exists today. Rank by how often they appear.
2. **Why current solutions fail** — per alternative (incumbent products, competitor brands, the status-quo behaviour): what specifically lets people down.
3. **Desired outcomes / unmet needs** — what people say they *wish* existed; the "I just want…" statements.
4. **Customer language** — the exact words and phrases people use (this becomes ad and copy language — don't sanitise it).
5. **Emotional drivers** — the feelings under the functional complaint (frustration, guilt, embarrassment, FOMO, pride).
6. **Objections & hesitations** — why people don't switch, or what they fear about new options in the category.
7. **Triggers & switching moments** — what makes someone finally look for an alternative.
8. **Surprises** — anything that contradicts the brand's assumptions. Flag these explicitly; they're often the most valuable.

Quote real people. Two vivid verbatim complaints beat a paragraph of paraphrase.

---

## How findings feed the brand-data file

When this runs inside Mode A, distil the research into:
- **Section 2 — Why people buy & the transformation** → the before-state, emotional drivers, and verbatim triggers.
- **Section 9 — Objections → rebuttals** → the hesitations and switching fears.
- **Section 10 — Market** → pain points, unmet needs, and the voice-of-customer evidence subsections.
- **Section 11 — Competitors** → the "weaknesses" / "how we win" lines, now backed by real complaints.
- **Section 12 — ICP** → the language and pre-purchase questions personas actually use.
- **Section 15 — SEO / content territory** → the literal questions people ask (answer-engine targets).

Always keep the source + confidence trail in **Section 18**.

---

## Standalone report output

When the user wants the trainable report (or always, in Mode A, save one alongside the brand-data file), structure it as:

1. **Executive summary** — the 3–5 insights that matter most, up top.
2. **The category & who's in it** — short framing + the alternatives consumers currently choose between.
3. **Market pain points** — ranked, each with 1–3 verbatim quotes and the source.
4. **Why current solutions fail** — by alternative, evidence-backed.
5. **What consumers actually want** — the unmet needs and desired outcomes, in their words.
6. **Where `[product type]` fits** — the gap it fills that alternatives don't; the wedge.
7. **Customer language bank** — pull-quotes and phrases the marketing team can lift directly into copy.
8. **Emotional drivers & objections** — what to lean into, what to pre-empt.
9. **Opportunities & angles** — concrete positioning and campaign angles the research surfaces.
10. **Sources** — where the insight came from, with links where possible, and a note on confidence/recency.

Write it so a marketer who wasn't in the room can pick it up and write on-message copy the same day. Real insight, plainly stated, with the receipts.
