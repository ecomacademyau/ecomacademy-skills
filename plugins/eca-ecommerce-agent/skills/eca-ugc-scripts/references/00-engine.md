# The Engine — shared rules for every UGC script

Read this once per session. It holds what's true for all seven video types: the spine, the hook engine, the voice rules, timing, the output format, and shooting notes. Each type's reference file builds on this — it won't repeat what's here.

## The spine: grab → hold → act

Every short-form video does three jobs in order. If any one fails, the video fails.

1. **Grab (0–3s) — the hook.** Stop the scroll. Specific enough to stop *this* person, universal enough that a thousand people feel "that's me." If the first line sounds like an ad, you've lost.
2. **Hold (the middle) — earn the watch.** Keep tension alive with *specifics* — real moments, real numbers, real objects, a story that's going somewhere. Vague = skip. This is where most videos die.
3. **Act (the end) — one ask.** Name one person's exact situation, then tell them the single thing to do. A friend's recommendation, not a sales close.

The whole craft is: open a loop fast, hold it open with concrete detail, close it with one clear action.

## The hook engine

The hook is the hardest line and the highest-leverage one. Never write one and ship it — generate a spread, vary the risk, test.

**Hook rules (apply to every type):**
- Lives in the first 0–3 seconds; it's a real sentence a person would say out loud.
- NEVER open with "Hi, I'm…", "Have you ever…", "Introducing…", or a feature.
- The strongest hooks lead with a **confession, a contrarian truth, a small embarrassment, a specific number, or a callout of the exact person**.
- Specific beats polished. "I ate a cold lunch at my desk every day for two years" beats "Tired of bad lunches?".
- No price in the hook. No corporate words.

**Hook generation prompt (adapt the bracketed inputs from `brand-data.md` / `config.md`):**

> You are an elite DTC short-form ad strategist. Generate **10 distinct hooks** (just the first 3 seconds, verbatim, exactly as the person would say them) for a **[VIDEO TYPE]** video.
> Brand context — Brand: [BRAND NAME]; Hero product: [PRODUCT — what it does]; Founder: [NAME — vibe] (if relevant); Category frustration: [SPECIFIC FRUSTRATION]; Audience: [WHO BUYS IT]; Real customer language: [VERBATIM PHRASES].
> For each hook give: (1) the hook in plain English; (2) one line on why it stops the scroll (name the psychological trigger); (3) a **spice rating 1–5** (how raw/vulnerable/risky it sounds).
> Rules: no corporate language; no "have you ever"; no "Hi I'm"; no price; every hook a real human sentence said at a dinner table; vary the spice — at least 2 should feel slightly uncomfortable. Then recommend the 3 to ship first and why.

**Always present 3 hooks to shoot: a safe one, a medium one, and one that's slightly uncomfortable.** Film a version with each; let the numbers pick the winner.

## Voice rules

- Write **spoken**, not written — contractions, fragments, the way they actually talk.
- One idea per line. Short. Breathable.
- Concrete nouns and real detail over adjectives and hype.
- If a line sounds like a brand wrote it, cross it out and say it again like you're talking to a mate. Keep that version.
- Match the brand's voice from `brand-data.md` (tone, words it leans on, words it bans). Never use a banned phrase.
- Don't over-claim. Only state product facts the brand profile says are safe; route around anything unverified.

## Timing discipline

- **~30s is the guide, not a ceiling.** Most paid/organic lands well at 15–30s — but **stretch to 45–60s when a longer cut genuinely tells the story better**, especially **Founder Story** (and other narrative types like Interview). Length should serve the story and retention, never padding: if the longer version holds attention the whole way through, keep it; if any second sags, cut back toward 30s.
- Give a **rough second-count per beat** and a **total**. If it runs long, the fix is almost always the product-explanation beat — cut it to the steps.
- Read it aloud at normal pace to check the real runtime before shipping.

## Output format (every script)

Deliver, in this order:
1. **Type & why** — the video type chosen and one line on why it fits the goal.
2. **3 hooks to test** — labelled safe / medium / spicy, with spice ratings.
3. **The script** — spoken lines, one beat per block, each tagged with its rough seconds. Put **[on-screen text]**, **[b-roll / cutaway]**, and **[action]** notes in brackets so a creator can follow it. *(B-roll is type-dependent — pure talking-to-camera types like the Founder Ad Story use NO b-roll or cutaways; follow the type's own file.)*
4. **Total runtime** + a one-line note if it needs trimming.
5. **Shooting notes** — framing, number of takes (one per hook), and what to avoid.
6. **The ask** — the exact CTA line and where it sends people.

Keep it copy-paste ready, equally usable as a self-shoot guide or a creator brief. Offer to save it as a file for handoff.

## After approval — export to Word (standing rule)
**Every time a member approves a script, export the final version to a Word (.docx) document — automatically, without being asked.** Use the docx skill. Include: the title, the brief (type · funnel stage · avatar · offer/angle · destination), the 3 hook options, and the **script as clean, editable spoken lines in sequence — NO beat headings or timing labels** (the doc is a writing surface for the member to refine into their own voice, so it should read like the words they'll actually say). Keep only essential on-screen/action cues as light notes. Then add total runtime, shoot notes, and the CTA below the script as reference. Save it to the member's folder and share the link. One script = one document; name it clearly (brand · type · angle).

## Universal shooting notes (carry into every type unless the type overrides)

- Phone, front or rear camera, natural light. Vertical 9:16.
- One take, talk-to-camera, no script in hand — like a voice note to a friend.
- Minimal/no music under talking hooks; let the words land.
- Capture the hook 3 ways (the 3 chosen hooks) in one sitting.
- B-roll: the product in real use, real environment, real hands. No staged stock look.
